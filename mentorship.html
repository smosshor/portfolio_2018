Peer Mentorship Research

2016-2018

I became deeply curious about the ways in which our virtual interactions with other people and machines help and hinder our learning. I conducted multiple research studies, with the guidance of researchers at Carnegie Mellon's HCII, investigating:

1. What factors lead to strong mentorship for students and mentors in an online environment?

2. How can we accurately track student motivation in a hybrid or online course, and what nudges can we utilize to maintain motivation?

3. How might we design human and machine powered tools and services to support students making decisions about their long-term goals and short-term choices?

What factors lead to strong mentorship for students and mentors in an online environment?

In the traditional classroom setting, mentoring is a highly effective means of increasing student retention. I explored the design of a system to support and facilitate mentorships between students engaged in online learning and peer mentors. This research project took about 3 months.

Initial Research

I interviewed with mentors and mentees seperately, both on the ways in which they interact with their mentor/ mentee, and their perceptions of their relationship over time, and also observed the mentor-pairs in action by having them place a small camera with audio recording to capture their meetings. I focused on "real life" interactions, while also asking about online interactions, which were definitely a key part of their relationships. Mentor pairs were typically already using Facebook Messager or texting to communicate to coordinate meetings and sometimes talk about things throughout a week, which led to different issues. Mentors sometimes reported feeling overwhelmed by a constant wave of issues. Key insights from this research were the importance of rapport in establishing a relationship where mentors could feel comfortable asking questions and feeling "stupid", and the development of a relationship over a quite significant period of time. I also became curious about what "matches" were "better" than others, for example was it better to be more or less similar.
